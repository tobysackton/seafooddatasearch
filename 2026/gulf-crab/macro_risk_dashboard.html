Iran war disruption (Feb 28 –): US-Israel strikes on Iran have choked oil shipments through the Strait of Hormuz. Oil prices surged 25%+, driving diesel from $3.90 to $4.86/gal in one week. This directly raises transportation costs for seafood distribution. Diesel is now at the highest level since the 2022 post-Ukraine spike.
Consumer Sentiment 56.4 (Jan 2026) — well below pre-pandemic avg of ~96. Will likely fall further on gas price shock.
Food at Home CPI 324.1 (Jan 2026) — up 33% from Jan 2020. Energy costs will feed through to food prices.
CAD/USD Exchange 1.356 (Mar 11) — CAD strengthening sharply from 1.44 in Feb. Petrocurrency effect: oil surge benefits Canada as a secure energy supplier. CAD outperforming EUR, GBP. Stronger CAD squeezes processor margins in USD terms.
Diesel $4.86/gal (Mar 9) — up $0.96 in one week. Iran war / Strait of Hormuz closure. Highest since 2022.
The macro picture has shifted significantly in the past two weeks. The Iran war introduces two competing pressures on Canadian crab pricing. Negative for processors: diesel costs raise transportation expense across the supply chain, and a strengthening CAD (from 1.44 to 1.36) compresses processor margins — they receive USD for crab but pay costs in an appreciating Canadian dollar. Negative for demand: rising fuel prices will further erode consumer sentiment, which was already weak at 56.4. Gas prices above $4 historically correlate with reduced restaurant traffic and discretionary grocery spending — both direct channels for crab. Partially offsetting: Canada's petrocurrency status means it is outperforming other G7 currencies (EUR, GBP) against the USD, reflecting Canada's position as a secure energy supplier. This dynamic is new as of early March and bears watching through the production season.
Source: Seafood Datasearch from FRED, EIA, Bank of Canada. Diesel and CAD/USD updated through March 9-11, 2026.
